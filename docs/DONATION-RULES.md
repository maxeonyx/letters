# NZ Electoral Donation Rules

Research needed on how donations to MPs work in New Zealand.

## Key Questions

1. Can you donate directly to individual MPs (not parties)?
2. What are the disclosure thresholds?
3. Are there limits on donation amounts?
4. What are the rules for anonymous donations?
5. Does donating via cash-in-envelope have different rules?

## Max's Position on Donation Hassle

If receiving an unexpected donation creates a small administrative hassle for the MP (e.g., they need to file something), that's acceptable - it draws attention to the letter, which is part of the goal.

## Findings

*Research conducted 2026-01-31 using Electoral Act 1993 (as at 01 January 2026)*

### Summary for Max's Use Case ($20-50, named donor)

**Good news:** Small donations with your name attached are straightforward and legal. At $20-50:
- Well under all disclosure thresholds
- No reporting burden for the MP
- No anonymous-donation complications
- Legal as long as you're not an overseas person

**The key distinction:** The Electoral Act regulates *candidate donations* (for election campaigns) and *party donations*. Sending money to a sitting MP *personally* (not for campaign use) isn't an "electoral donation" under the Act - but it may trigger Parliament's gift/pecuniary interest rules and could look like bribery if framed wrong.

### A. Can You Donate to an Individual MP?

**Three different things:**

1. **Candidate donation** (Electoral Act s207) - money "for use in the candidate's campaign for election." This applies when they're actively a candidate (election period). Goes to their campaign account.

2. **Party donation** - money "made to a party" or "intended for the benefit of the party." Goes via party secretary.

3. **Personal gift to sitting MP** - Not regulated by Electoral Act, but governed by Parliament's Standing Orders on pecuniary interests. MPs must declare gifts over certain thresholds.

**Electorate vs List MPs:**
- Electorate MPs have campaign accounts and may accept candidate donations during elections
- List MPs are candidates too (on the party list) but donations for their campaign would typically go via party
- Outside election periods, neither has an obvious "donate to me" mechanism

### B. Disclosure Thresholds

**Candidate donations** (s209):
- Must disclose donations **exceeding $1,500** (aggregated per donor per campaign)
- Required: donor name, address, amount, date

**Party donations** (s210):
- Must disclose donations **exceeding $6,000** (aggregated per donor per year)
- Required: donor name, address, amount, date

**At $20-50:** No disclosure required. The MP has zero paperwork.

### C. Anonymous Donations

Since Max will use his real name, this section is FYI only:

**Anonymous candidate donations** (s207I):
- If >$1,500: candidate must pay excess to Electoral Commission within 20 working days
- If >$50 and suspected overseas person: must pay excess to Electoral Commission

**Anonymous party donations:**
- Similar rules, higher threshold ($1,500)

**At $20-50 with name attached:** None of this applies.

### D. Overseas Person Rules

**Important constraint** (s207K): Overseas donations may not exceed $50.

- If you're a NZ citizen/resident: no issue
- If donation >$50 and from overseas person: must be returned or paid to Electoral Commission
- Candidate/party must take "reasonable steps" to verify donor isn't overseas (s207JA)

**Max is in NZ:** No issue.

### E. Election vs Non-Election Periods

**During elections:**
- "Candidate donations" apply when someone is formally a candidate
- Reporting tied to candidate returns filed after the election

**Outside elections:**
- Party donations still regulated (annual returns)
- Donations to individual MPs aren't really a defined category
- Money sent to an MP personally would be a "gift" under Parliament rules, not an electoral donation

### F. When Would This Be Illegal or Problematic?

1. **Overseas person donating >$50** - illegal under Electoral Act

2. **Bribery/corruption** (Crimes Act 1961, s103) - It's an offence to give money to an MP "with intent to influence" their actions in Parliament. The framing matters enormously.
   - BAD: "I'm giving you money so you'll raise this issue" 
   - OK: "I'm donating to support your work; separately, here's an issue I care about"
   - The critical review flagged this: the letter template's "I enclosed a donation as a token of the seriousness" is borderline

3. **Perception of quid-pro-quo** - Even if legal, staff may bin it if it reads like "pay for access"

4. **Parliament pecuniary interest rules** - MPs must declare gifts. A $20-50 gift probably falls under threshold but we should confirm the exact rules (Parliament website was blocked during research).

### G. Practical Recommendations

1. **Use your real name and address** - avoids all anonymous-donation complications

2. **Decouple the donation from the ask** - Don't frame it as "I'm paying for you to listen." Frame as:
   - "I support your work representing [electorate/NZ]" (separate paragraph)
   - "I'm writing about an issue I believe is critical..." (main content)

3. **Keep it small** - $20-50 is clearly "gesture" territory, not influence

4. **Cash in envelope is fine** - Legal, simple, under all thresholds

5. **Consider: donate to electorate campaign during election period** - More standard mechanism, clearer purpose
