## Stakeholders

- Max, trying to communicate a serious concern to MPs in a way that is principled, useful, and actually worth sending.
- MPs and their staff, who need a short, credible, readable letter with a clear reason to pay attention.
- Other people working on AI safety in New Zealand, who should not be undermined, misrepresented, or crowded out by this project.

## Stakeholder Stories

Max wants to send letters that preserve what he finds important about the project while still being persuasive to MPs who are not already deep in AI policy.

Max wants the letters to argue for international coordination and share a framework for thinking about AI in New Zealand, especially the idea that keeping the future human is a national priority in an increasingly automated world.

Max wants the letters to acknowledge existential risk concerns without slipping into language that feels gratuitously alarmist.

Max wants to avoid presenting himself as the authority on this issue, and instead point MPs toward other credible people, groups, events, and resources in New Zealand.

Max wants the project to be worth doing in practice: it should have a plausible path to impact, and it should not interfere with other people working on AI safety in New Zealand.

Max wants a practical sending setup: printing, paper, envelopes, postage or freepost details, and a clear operational workflow.

## Detailed Stories

### Letter purpose

An MP receives the letter and can quickly understand that this is a serious informational intervention, not a stunt, not a demand for a favor, and not a claim of personal expertise.

An MP receives the letter and sees a small but real ask: keep an open mind, treat AI as a fast-moving issue, and support New Zealand engagement in international coordination and AI control measures.

### New Zealand framing

An MP can understand the argument that, in an increasingly automated world, New Zealand has a national interest in keeping the future human.

An MP can understand that this is not only about abstract global risk; it is also about New Zealand's place in a world where AI may change work, income, and the human basis of the economy.

### Voice and credibility

The letters should not overclaim Max's authority.

The letters should instead direct MPs to other people and institutions working on this issue in New Zealand, such as the Christchurch AI Safety Conference and potentially Good Ancestors New Zealand if that starts soon.

The project should likely have a links page / website that can be referenced from the letters.

### Paths to impact

The project should be evaluated against these possible paths to impact, in decreasing order of likelihood:

1. We get a line on a summary presented to the MP about the letters sent in this week.
2. They actually read it and take something in.
3. They follow up in some capacity with the ideas or the links we've presented.

### Delivery and operations

The project needs a practical plan for printing and mailing physical letters, including nice paper, double-sided printing, and envelopes.

The project needs to confirm the mailing details, including whether most letters can be sent freepost and what exceptions apply.

## Current Constraints

- Remove mention of money from the letters and from the core strategy.
- Stay serious, but not dramatic.
- Be clear and concise.
- Have some kind of ask, even if the ask is not very strong.
- Preserve openness to fast change: AI may be a different issue again by election day.
